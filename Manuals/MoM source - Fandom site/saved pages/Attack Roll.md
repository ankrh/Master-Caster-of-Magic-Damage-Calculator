An **Attack Roll** is a series of d10 rolls made to determine how much unmitigated [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** (or Hits) an attack will inflict. This may then be reduced by the target's [Defense](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Defense "Defense") **Armor** or other factors.

The term "d10" originates from classic role-playing games, where
"d" stands for "die" or "dice", and the number is used to denote the
amount of sides on this dice. In computer gaming, a roll is a randomly
generated number within certain confines (i.e. a roll of the figurative
dice). Thus, a d10 roll is, in essence, a randomly generated integer
between 1 and 10.

The amount of dice being "rolled" corresponds to the strength of the attack (referred to as its [Attack Strength](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Attack_Strength "Attack Strength")).
Each die is individually evaluated to determine whether it is a
"success" or a "failure". Disregarding any modifiers, a roll is
considered "successful" if its result is 3 or lower (or, alternatively, 8
or higher). This corresponds to a **30% success chance**, which is called the base chance [To Hit](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit "To Hit") **To Hit**.

The actual result of an **Attack Roll**
is its amount of "successes" (also called "succesful hits"), which is
always a sum between 0 and the Attack Strength. This is the value that
is passed on to the next stage of the [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** resolution process: mitigation.

The term **Attack Roll** is sometimes also used to refer to the individual dice cast during this process. However, as [Defense Rolls](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Defense_Roll "Defense Roll") are triggered only after all of the attack dice in the *series* have been rolled and evaluated, this article considers the entire series to be a single **Attack Roll**.

## Concept

**Attack Rolls** have been
an integral part of role-playing games since the dawn of their era.
Whenever a creature or character tries to hit something (most often
another creature or character) with a sword or spell, some method is
called for to determine whether they succeed in their endeavour. This is
commonly implemented as an **Attack Roll**.

In the d20 system, for example, which draws its roots from
Dungeons and Dragons, an attack roll is made using a 20-sided dice, with
the opponent’s defense score (called Armor Class there) used as a
target number for success. In that system, defense is a passive
attribute, and does not have its own rolls. The system used in MoM is
different in two very important ways.

First, potential damage is a linear function of the [Attack Strength](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Attack_Strength "Attack Strength").
In d20, no damage can be dealt if the attack roll is below the target
number (the attack simply misses), and the actual damage is determined
by a separate die roll instead. In contrast, MoM makes a roll for *every single point of the Attack Strength*, with each roll representing an individual source of potential damage.

Second, defense provides a linear function for *damage reduction*. Instead of it being a passive attribute, that is only used to determine the target number for attack rolls; [Defense](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Defense "Defense") in this game is also an active characteristic. A roll is made for *every single point of Defense*, with each of them representing a source of potential damage *reduction*. This contrasts d20's use of Armor as a damage *avoidance* statistic instead.

There are two basic ways to conceptualize the attack process. The
first, arguably simpler method, is to consider each point of Attack
Strength as a separate attempt at dealing damage. This may be easier to
understand, but other mechanics suggest that this is not the way the
developers intended it to work. Instead, the whole series of rolls (one
for each point of Attack Strength) is meant to represent a single
attempt at causing damage, with the result setting out the magnitude of
the hit (before any reductions).

## Effect

**Attack Rolls** are the second step in the resolution of any conventional [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** attack. They are involved in all regular-, and most special attacks, that a unit can make: [Melee Attack](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Melee_Attack "Melee Attack") **Melee Attacks**, all [Ranged Missile Attack](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Ranged_Missile_Attack "Ranged Missile Attack") [Ranged Boulder Attack](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Ranged_Boulder_Attack "Ranged Boulder Attack") [Ranged Magical Attack](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Ranged_Magical_Attack "Ranged Magical Attack") **Ranged Attacks**, [Thrown Attack](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Thrown_Attack "Thrown Attack") **Thrown Attacks**, [Fire Breath](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Fire_Breath "Fire Breath") [Lightning Breath](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Lightning_Breath "Lightning Breath") **Breath Attacks**, and, to a certain extent, [Gaze Attacks](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Gaze_Attack "Gaze Attack"). They are also used to determine direct [Ranged Magical Attack](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Ranged_Magical_Attack "Ranged Magical Attack") **Spell Damage** (both normal and [Area Damage](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Area_Damage "Area Damage")).

On the other hand, **Attack Rolls** play no role in resolving the [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** caused by [Death](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Death "Death")**[Life Drain](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Life_Drain "Life Drain")** and other [Life Steal](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Life_Steal "Life Steal") [Life Stealing](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Life_Steal "Life Steal") attacks; [Poison Touch](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Poison_Touch "Poison Touch") [Poison Touch](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Poison_Touch "Poison Touch"); or any other [Resistance](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Resistance "Resistance") **Resistance**-based spell or [Special Attack](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Special_Attack "Special Attack"). They are also omitted for attacks that deal [Doom-](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Doom_Damage "Doom Damage")/[Chaos](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Chaos_(Item_Power) "Chaos (Item Power)") Damage, including any attacks made against units affected by [Death](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Death "Death")**[Black Sleep](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Black_Sleep "Black Sleep")**. Naturally, effects that make no **Attack Roll** may also not be defended against using [Defense Rolls](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Defense_Roll "Defense Roll") (but will often involve [Resistance](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Resistance "Resistance") **Resistance** rolls instead).

**Attack Rolls** convert *potential damage* ([Attack Strength](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Attack_Strength "Attack Strength")) into actual, *unmitigated* [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage**
using random "dice rolls". As such, they have two important
characteristics besides the type of the dice being used: the amount of
dice that needs to be rolled, and the method for evaluating the result.
The former is determined by the strength of the attack, while the latter
is based on whether each individual die was rolled "successfully" or
not.

### Attack Strength

:   *Main article: [Attack Strength](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Attack_Strength "Attack Strength")*

The strength of an attack sets out the number of "dice" used for an **Attack Roll**. Thus, a creature with an **Attack Strength** of [Melee Attack](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Melee_Attack "Melee Attack") **1** will only get a single die, while a spell with a strength of [Ranged Magical Attack](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Ranged_Magical_Attack "Ranged Magical Attack") **15** will roll 15 dice. This is why **Attack Rolls** are only the second step in resolving attacks: they have to be preceded by the determination of the **Attack Strength**.

Each dice is individually evaluated to be either a "success" or a "failure" based on the number rolled, and the result of the **Attack Roll** is the amount of "successes" generated. This will always be between 0 and the **Attack Strength**, and corresponds to the raw [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** delivered by the attack (before any mitigation).

### Success Chance

The base chance for each "dice" of an **Attack Roll**
to succeed is 30%, which can be visualized either as the results 1, 2,
and 3; or the results 8, 9, and 10; of the 10-sided "dice". While
technically the same, the difference may make understanding [To Hit](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit "To Hit") **to hit** modifiers easier.

All spells cast by [Wizards](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Wizard "Wizard") and [Heroes](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Hero "Hero") use the base 30% success chance for their **Attack Rolls**, and this may not be altered by any means. The [Immolation](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Immolation "Immolation") [Immolation](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Immolation "Immolation")
ability also uses this value, despite any modifiers active on the unit
possessing it. It is always considered to be a spell attack, rather than
one made by the unit.

### "To Hit" modifiers

:   *Main article: [To Hit](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit "To Hit")*

When performing attacks (whether [Melee](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Melee_Attack "Melee Attack"), [Ranged](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Ranged_Attack "Ranged Attack"), or [Special](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Special_Attack "Special Attack")); most [fantastic creatures](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Fantastic_Unit "Fantastic Unit"), and experienced [Normal Units](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Normal_Unit "Normal Unit") and [Heroes](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Hero "Hero") *do not actually use the base success chance*. Instead, their rolls are influenced by a separate statistic called a [To Hit](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit "To Hit") **To Hit modifier**; to represent a higher (or, in the case of [cursed](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Unit_Curse "Unit Curse") units, an unnaturally low) accuracy.

Going by the dual visualization given above, [To Hit](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit "To Hit") **To Hit modifiers**
either apply to the "target number" of the rolls (in the first case),
or the result of the rolls (in the second case). Take, for instance, a
modifier of [To Hit](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit "To Hit") **+1 [To Hit](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit "To Hit")**.
If the rolls succeeded on 1, 2, and 3, the modifier would be added to
the target number for success, yielding successes on 1, 2, 3, and 4
instead. On the other hand, if rolls succeeded on an 8 or higher, the
modifier would be added directly to the rolled number, making a roll of 7
also a success.

Both visualizations lead to the same result in terms of statistical chance of success. In fact, on this wiki, [To Hit](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit "To Hit") **To Hit modifiers**
are generally expressed as percentage numbers for easier understanding,
in which case they simply modify the chance for success by the amount
indicated. However, it is important to note that the actual rolls are *not percentages*, and in the case of such representation, *only whole multiples of 10% will be used* in practice (i.e. there is no 37% chance to hit, only 30% or 40%).

:   Modifiers [To Hit](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit "To Hit") **To Hit**, both positive and negative, can come from a multitude of sources, ranging from [experience](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Experience_Level "Experience Level") and weapon quality; through [abilities](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Unit_Ability "Unit Ability")
    and spells; to distance modifiers for ranged attacks. A comprehensive
    list of sources and their respective modifiers can be found in the ["To Hit Modifiers"](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit#To_Hit_Modifiers "To Hit") section of the main article. In addition, an overview table is located in the [following section](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit#Table_of_To_Hit_Modifiers "To Hit") (excluding [Ranged](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit#Ranged_Penalties "To Hit") and [Counter Attack penalties](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/To_Hit#Counterattack_Penalty "To Hit")).

## Dealing Damage

:   *Main article: [Physical Damage](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Physical_Damage "Physical Damage")*
:   *See also: [Defense Roll](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Defense_Roll "Defense Roll"), [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points")*

As noted already, **Attack Rolls** are the second step in resolving an attack (after determining effective [Attack Strength](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Attack_Strength "Attack Strength")).
They are normally only performed once during the process. This may
sound confusing, as more often than not an "Attack" command given to a
unit will involve multiple **Attack Rolls**.
However, each of these is always part of a separate damage resolution
process, revolving solely around a single one of these rolls. Even in
the case of [Area Damage](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Area_Damage "Area Damage"), where each [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figure** in a unit is attacked individually, the resolution is separate up to the point of summing up and applying the [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage**. This is akin to the simultaneous delivery of [Melee Damage](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Melee_Damage "Melee Damage"), where neither combatant suffers any until the amount they deal has also been calculated.

The entire process looks something like this:

| Stage | Step | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Attack** | **1** | Determine (situational) [Attack Strength](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Attack_Strength "Attack Strength"). |
| **2** | Using this value, make an **Attack Roll** to determine raw (unmitigated) [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage**. |
|  | | |
| Defense | **3** | Calculate effective [Defense](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Defense "Defense") **Defense**. |
| **4** | Make a [Defense Roll](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Defense_Roll "Defense Roll"). |
| **5** | Subtract the result of the roll from the raw [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage**. |
|  | | |
| Excess | **6** | If the remainder is greater than the maximum [Hit Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Hit_Points "Hit Points") **Hit Points** of the [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figure** being attacked, another [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figure** will also be hit. Store the maximum [Hit Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Hit_Points "Hit Points") **Hit Point** value as actual [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** (the first time this happens), or add it to the already accumulated number (on subsequent occasions). Reduce the raw [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** by this maximum [Hit Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Hit_Points "Hit Points") **Hit Point** value and jump back to step #4. |
|  | | |
| Resolve | **7** | Add any remainder (this will be less than or equal to the target [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figure**'s maximum [Hit Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Hit_Points "Hit Points") **Hit Points**, and can also be zero) to any [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** accumulated by step #6: this will be the total [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** done to the target *unit*. |
| **8** | Apply the [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** to the unit as a whole, and recalculate remaining [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figures** based on unit health. This concludes the attack. |

Area Damage is different from this in that it never deals excess [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage**, and instead discards anything above the [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figure**'s current [Hit Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Hit_Points "Hit Points") **Hit Points** in step #6, simply storing the remaining value as actual [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage**. On the other hand, Area Damage resolution also executes steps #1 through #6 for all [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figures** of the target unit before moving on to steps #7 and #8 to sum up and apply the [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage**.

### Multi-Figure Units

In a [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure#Multi-Figure_Units "Figure") **Multi-Figure Unit**, each separate [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figure** gets to make an attack whenever the unit as a whole performs one. Each of these [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figures** individually possesses the same [Attack Strength](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Attack_Strength "Attack Strength")
as the entire unit, and each attack is handled as a separate process.
This has two major consequences. On one hand, it means that the total [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage**
potential of such a unit is not quite its Attack Strength, but rather,
it is this attribute multiplied by the amount of remaining [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figures**. Thus, more [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figures** obviously means more potential [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage**.

On the other hand, however, because each [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figure** attacks separately, the *actual* [Damage Points](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Damage_Points "Damage Points") **damage** is generally much lower in practice than the direct product of the Attack Strength and [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figure** count would indicate. This is because each **Attack Roll**
represents a separate source of raw damage, and as such these can also
be reduced individually by the defender, who gets to make a [Defense Roll](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Defense_Roll "Defense Roll") against every single attack.

Unfortunately, this is in direct contradiction to what is stated in the *Game Manual*, which claims that only one **Attack Roll** is made using the product of the Attack Strength and [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **figure** count. That information is incorrect, the game actually performs a separate attack for every [Figure](https://masterofmagic.fandom.com/wiki/Figure "Figure") **Figure**.
